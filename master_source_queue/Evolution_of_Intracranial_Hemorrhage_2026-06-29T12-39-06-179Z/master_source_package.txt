## Fused Teaching Backbone

### Definition And Mechanism

- [Both] Epidural hematoma is blood between the inner table of the skull and the outer periosteal layer of dura.
- [Both] The classic acute traumatic EDH is a hyperdense biconvex/lentiform extraaxial collection on noncontrast CT, usually at the coup site and usually adjacent to a skull fracture.
- [Both] Most EDHs are arterial, usually from fracture-related laceration near the middle meningeal artery groove; venous EDHs are less common and occur near dural venous sinuses.
- [STATdx] The swirl sign is hypodense active/rapid bleeding with unretracted clot; CTA may show a spot/dot sign or active extravasation, and contrast leakage predicts expansion.
- [Both] EDH can coexist with subdural/subarachnoid hemorrhage or contusions, so the search pattern should not stop once the epidural collection is found.

### Imaging Pattern

- [Both] Best first-line study in trauma is noncontrast head CT with bone windows. Use bone CT to identify the fracture and coronal/sagittal reformats when vertex or skull-base EDH is possible.
- [Both] Classic arterial EDH is unilateral, supratentorial, temporoparietal more often than frontal/parietooccipital, biconvex, and limited by sutures.
- [Both] EDH compresses the underlying brain and subarachnoid space; displaced dura can form a hyperdense medial margin on CT or a black line between EDH and brain on MR.
- [Both] Acute EDH is commonly hyperdense; mixed density or a central low-density swirl sign should raise concern for active bleeding and rapid expansion.
- [STATdx] Air within an EDH suggests communication with a paranasal sinus, mastoid air cells, or skull-base fracture, and the adjacent bone-window image should be reviewed.
- [STATdx] CTA/CTV is useful when venous sinus injury, active extravasation, sinus compression, thrombus, or a venous EDH near a dural sinus is suspected.
- [Both] MR may show the displaced dura on T1/T2 and is helpful when the collection straddles dural compartments or when venous sinus displacement is the teaching question.

### Boundary Rules And Exceptions

- [Both] Arterial EDHs usually do not cross sutures.
- [Both] Exceptions include venous EDH, sutural diastasis/fracture, large hematoma with diastatic fracture, and pediatric cases.
- [STATdx] Venous EDH may straddle sutures or dural attachments and can cross the falx or tentorium; the dural sinus is displaced or compressed rather than necessarily occluded.
- [Both] Vertex EDH can be subtle and easily missed; coronal reconstructions and venous sinus attention are important.
- [Both] The CT comma sign indicates coexisting EDH and SDH and matters because the two surgical compartments may require separate treatment planning.

### Differential Diagnosis

- [Both] Acute subdural hematoma is usually crescentic, crosses sutures, and is limited by dural reflections, but EDH and SDH may coexist and either can occasionally mimic the other's shape.
- [Both] Extraaxial neoplasm such as meningioma, lymphoma, or metastasis can mimic a biconvex epidural process when hemorrhagic or hyperdense.
- [Both] Infection/inflammation, including epidural empyema, tuberculosis, and neurosarcoidosis, enters the differential when the clinical context is nontraumatic or chronic.
- [Both] Extramedullary hematopoiesis is rare and should require the appropriate blood dyscrasia context.
- [STATdx] MR demonstration of a hypointense dural line between the EDH and brain is a useful recognition feature separating EDH from subdural hematoma.

### Clinical Course And Management

- [Both] The classic lucid interval occurs in about half of cases but is not required.
- [Both] Delayed development or enlargement is common within the first 36 hours; repeat CT is important when conservative management is chosen.
- [Both] Prompt recognition and treatment generally produce good outcomes, while delayed diagnosis, referral, or operation worsens prognosis.
- [Both] Most arterial EDHs are surgically evacuated, especially large, mixed-density, expanding, or mass-effect lesions.
- [Both] Conservative management can be considered for selected patients with GCS 13-15, EDH thickness below 40 mm, less than 6 mm midline shift, no other surgical lesion, and close repeat imaging.
- [Both] Some EDHs under 1 cm without edema may be managed nonoperatively; anterior middle cranial fossa venous EDHs are often observed.
- [Both] Endovascular middle meningeal artery occlusion is a minimally invasive option in selected nonsurgical acute EDH cases.
- [STATdx] Posterior fossa EDH carries higher mortality and may present later because venous expansion can be slower; mass effect on posterior fossa structures raises urgency.
