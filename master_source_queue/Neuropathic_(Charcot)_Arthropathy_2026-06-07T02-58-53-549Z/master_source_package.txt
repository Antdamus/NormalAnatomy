=== CURATED MASTER CONTENT ===

Terminology and definition:
- RadPrimer: Charcot equals neuropathic joint; a severely and rapidly destructive joint process, with etiology often suggested by location.
- STATdx: progressive joint destruction secondary to long-term polyneuropathy. Use this wording when teaching the mechanism, but keep RadPrimer as the canonical label.

Core imaging spine:
- Teach the multiple Ds / 5 Ds: normal or patient-appropriate bone density, joint distension, bony debris, cartilage or joint destruction, and joint disorganization/dislocation/deformity. STATdx also emphasizes increased density/sclerosis as part of the D pattern.
- Location suggests etiology: shoulder and wrist can reflect syringomyelia; wrist and foot/ankle commonly diabetes; spine follows spinal cord injury, tabes, or diabetes, especially mobile segments caudad to a stabilized segment; hip suggests alcohol or tabes; knee suggests tabes, congenital pain indifference/insensitivity, or steroid injection; ankle/foot strongly suggests diabetes and often involves Lisfranc/TMT before talonavicular, intertarsal, Chopart, tibiotalar, or subtalar joints.
- Morphology: hypertrophic form has prominent debris (classically knee); atrophic form has resorbed debris and is common in diabetic ankle/foot; combined forms are also common.
- Rate of destruction can be very fast and may rival septic arthritis. Rapid hip destruction can mimic rapidly destructive osteoarthritis unless neuropathic etiology is considered.

Mechanism and pathology:
- Use RadPrimer as backbone: altered sympathetic control of osseous blood flow may cause hyperemia and active bone resorption; blunted pain/proprioception allows relaxation of supporting structures, chronic instability, recurrent microtrauma under abnormal loading, osseous fragmentation, and joint disorganization.
- Use STATdx supplemental phrasing: polyneuropathy drives decreased pain sensation and proprioception; abnormal vascular innervation and microrepetitive trauma create osteoclast/osteoblast imbalance and osteolysis/bone resorption.
- Gross/surgical feature from both sources: significant cartilaginous and osseous debris within synovial membrane.

Imaging modality teaching:
- Radiographs are central for diagnosis and long-term surveillance. Early disease may look normal or like osteoarthritis, with subtle subluxation, osteopenia, arch collapse in foot, or mixed cystic/erosion-like and productive changes. Middle/late disease shows debris, dislocation/subluxation, disorganization, large effusions, bone proliferation, sclerosis, and deformity.
- CT is not usually required for diagnosis but is excellent for proving articular destruction, fragmentation, subluxation, and debris location; it can prevent undercalling TMT/Lisfranc destruction and can show massive debris-containing effusions that mimic mineralized masses.
- MR is for early detection and problem solving. It shows marrow edema, subchondral fracture lines, effusion, soft-tissue edema, debris, intraarticular bodies, destruction, and disorganization. In the infected Charcot foot problem, T1 and postcontrast images are more useful than fluid-sensitive sequences. Confluent low T1 signal, gas/sinus tract to abnormal bone, abscess, and less debris favor superimposed osteomyelitis; hazy/reticular low T1 signal and fat replacement/reactive change favor neuropathic change. Significant overlap exists.

Classification and staging from STATdx:
- Sanders-Frykberg foot/ankle zones: Zone I intermetatarsal/MTP; Zone II TMT; Zone III tarsal; Zone IV ankle/subtalar; Zone V calcaneus.
- Eichenholtz radiologic staging: Stage 0 normal; Stage I dislocation/fragmentation/debris with or without osteopenia; Stage II sclerosis, coalescence, resorption of fine debris, and ankylosis of large fragments; Stage III well-corticated fragments, decreased sclerosis, and chronic osseous remodeling.

Differential and traps:
- Infection/septic joint/osteomyelitis can coexist with Charcot. Ulcer, gas, sinus tract to abnormal bone, abscess, and confluent T1 marrow replacement support infection; disc gas at the spine favors spinal neuroarthropathy. Aspiration may be needed in difficult spine cases.
- Sarcoma/chondrosarcoma trap: Charcot shoulder or other debris-containing effusions can mimic a soft-tissue mass or chondroid matrix. Charcot debris is osseous and intraarticular/effusion-based; sarcoma is typically extraarticular and matrix is not just floating articular debris.
- Osteoarthritis or inflammatory/metabolic arthropathy trap: early OA can resemble early Charcot, but Charcot has larger effusions, more prominent debris, dislocation/subluxation, and much faster destruction.
- Hip trap: initial radiograph may look like osteoarthritis, but rapid progression to debris, acetabular/femoral destruction, hatchet sign, and an etiology clue such as alcoholism/cirrhosis should trigger Charcot.

Clinical issues and treatment:
- Swelling, instability, deformity, numbness, paresthesia, and sometimes near-normal proprioception can occur; careful neurologic exam may reveal reduced deep pain/proprioception.
- Diabetes is a major modern cause, especially in lower limb; RadPrimer/STATdx state 15% of diabetic patients develop Charcot joints. STATdx notes more than 10-year diabetes history is a major risk factor. Twenty percent of syringomyelia patients develop Charcot joints; 10-20% of tabes dorsalis patients develop Charcot joints worldwide.
- Natural history: progressive and sometimes rapid destruction; worsening alignment increases risk of ulceration and eventual osteomyelitis.
- Treatment is multidisciplinary: control/prevent infection, manage ulcers, revascularize if clinically indicated, preserve function and mobility, reconstruction/arthrodesis if conservative care fails, amputation in refractory cases. RadPrimer notes arthrodesis is difficult and recurrence/major complications occur in about 25%.
