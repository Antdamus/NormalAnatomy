TITLE: Neuropathic (Charcot) Arthropathy

KEY FACTS
- Terminology
  - Progressive joint destruction secondary to long-term polyneuropathy
- Imaging
  - Best imaging clue: multiple Ds
    - Joint dislocation, deformity &/or disorganization
    - Bone debris
    - Cartilage/joint destruction
    - ↑ density (sclerosis)
    - Joint distention
  - Associated etiologies by location
    - Ankle/foot: diabetes mellitus, amyloidosis, meningomyelocele
    - Shoulder: syringomyelia (if suspected, consider MR of cervical spine)
    - Wrist: diabetes mellitus; syringomyelia (if suspected, consider MR of cervical spine)
    - Hip: alcoholism, tabes dorsalis
    - Knee: tabes dorsalis, congenital indifference or insensitivity to pain, steroid injection, amyloidosis
    - Spine: spinal cord injury, tabes dorsalis, diabetes mellitus
  - Radiography: important for diagnosis and long-term surveillance
  - MR: utility for early detection of disease and problem solving in setting of superimposed infection
- Clinical Issues
  - Diabetes mellitus is common in general population
    - 15% of diabetic patients develop Charcot joints, especially in lower limb (such as foot and ankle)
  - 20% of syringomyelia patients develop Charcot joints
  - Worldwide; 10-20% of patients with tabes dorsalis develop Charcot joints
- Diagnostic Checklist
  - Even though debris and other findings may be distant from joint, confirming primary process originates from joint makes diagnosis

TERMINOLOGY
- Synonyms
  - Charcot joint
  - Neuropathic osteoarthropathy; neuropathic joint disease
- Definitions
  - Progressive joint destruction secondary to long-term polyneuropathy

IMAGING
- General Features
  - Best diagnostic clue
    - Multiple Ds
      - Joint dislocation, deformity, &/or disorganization
      - Bone debris
      - Cartilage/joint destruction
      - ↑ density (sclerosis)
      - Joint distention
  - Location
    - Associated etiologies by location
      - Ankle/foot: diabetes mellitus, amyloidosis, meningomyelocele
        - Lisfranc (tarsal-metatarsal) > talonavicular > intertarsal > Chopart (hindfoot-midfoot), tibiotalar, subtalar
      - Shoulder: syringomyelia (if suspected, consider MR cervical spine)
      - Wrist: diabetes mellitus; syringomyelia (if suspected, consider MR cervical spine)
      - Hip: alcoholism, tabes dorsalis
      - Knee: tabes dorsalis, congenital indifference or insensitivity to pain, steroid injection, amyloidosis
      - Spine: spinal cord injury, tabes dorsalis, diabetes mellitus
        - Mobile segments caudal to stabilized segment of spine in paraplegic are at risk
  - Morphology
    - Hypertrophic (prominent bony debris): 20%, particularly in knees
    - Atrophic (mostly resorbed): 40%, particularly in diabetic ankle/foot
    - Combined hypertrophic and atrophic: 40%
- Radiographic Findings
  - Early-stage disease
    - May appear normal or resemble osteoarthritis (OA)
    - Osteopenia
    - Subtle subluxations (commonly Lisfranc joint at foot)
    - Collapse of longitudinal arch (at foot)
    - Mixed erosion-like cysts and productive changes
  - Middle- and late-stage disease
    - Prominent osseous debris
    - Prominent joint dislocation/subluxation, disorganization
    - Large joint effusions
      - May present as palpable soft tissue mass/prominent soft tissue density
      - May contain floating osseous debris, mimicking juxtaarticular or paraspinal mass
    - Prominent osteophyte formation, bone proliferation, ± bone consolidation
    - ± sclerosis
    - Prominent bone/joint deformity
- CT Findings
  - Similar to radiographic findings
  - Shows distended joint space containing osseous debris
    - Because of tremendous distension, debris within apparent mass may appear several centimeters distant from joint
    - Be careful not to misinterpret this appearance as tumor containing matrix (i.e., chondrosarcoma)
  - CT-reformatted images can show pathology in optimized planes
    - Improved visualization of bony proliferation and consolidation
  - In combination with PET, may be suitable alternative for monitoring of associated inflammatory and infection processes when MR is contraindicated
- MR Findings
  - Early-stage disease
    - Bone marrow edema [T1: intermediate to low signal intensity (SI); T2: high SI]
    - Soft tissue edema (T1 low, T2 high SI)
    - Joint effusion (T1 low, T2 high SI)
    - Subchondral fracture (low SI fracture lines with adjacent bone edema)
  - Middle- and late-stage disease
    - Same early-stage findings but more extensive and prominent
    - Cortex fractures, bone debris, bone proliferation
    - Joint destruction, dislocation/subluxation, disorganization, intraarticular bodies
    - Large effusions
      - May contain floating bone debris, mimicking juxtaarticular or paraspinal mass
    - Well-marginated subchondral cysts
  - Utility for monitoring disease progression
    - Detect resolution of bone marrow edema over time (potential positive development)
    - Detect appearance of new bone marrow edema over time (potential negative development)
  - Pitfall: potential overlap with imaging findings of osteomyelitis
    - Assess for additional signs of infection
      - To favor infection assess for associated soft tissue ulcer, gas, sinus tract, &/or abscess; and also discitis-osteomyelitis at spine
      - Disc gas favors spinal neuroarthropathy at spine
    - Gadolinium contrast: potentially controversial in some patient populations (e.g., renal failure, etc.)
- Imaging Recommendations
  - Best imaging tool
    - Radiography: preferred imaging technique since patient will need continuous monitoring overtime
    - MR: utility for early detection of disease and problem solving in setting of possible superimposed infection

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS
- Infection
  - Overlap of imaging findings and superimposed infection can complicate Charcot joint
    - Assess for associated soft tissue ulcer, gas, sinus tract, &/or abscess; and also discitis-osteomyelitis at spine
    - Disc gas favors spinal neuroarthropathy at spine
- Sarcoma
  - Large effusions with internal debris may mimic presentation of sarcoma on radiographs and physical examination
    - Sarcoma is typically extraarticular, as opposed to enlarged intraarticular-based effusions
    - Advanced cross-sectional anatomic and functional imaging techniques will be helpful toward making presumptive diagnosis of sarcoma
- Arthropathy (Osteoarthritis, Metabolic Arthropathy, Inflammatory Arthropathy)
  - Early-stage OA may resemble early Charcot joint
  - Bone debris in Charcot joint usually more prominent
  - End-stage cases of metabolic and inflammatory arthropathy can mimic bone and joint destruction and disorganization of advanced Charcot osteoarthropathy

PATHOLOGY
- General Features
  - Etiology
    - Primary pathogenesis uncertain
    - Polyneuropathy → ↓ pain sensation and proprioception → recurrent mechanical injury
      - Multifactorial: likely combination of long-term progressive microrepetitive trauma and abnormal vascular innervation
        - Neuromuscular abnormality → joint instability → abnormal joint loading → progressive joint destruction
        - Hyperemia → inflammatory response → osteoclast/osteoblast imbalance → osteolysis/bone resorption
- Staging, Grading, & Classification
  - Sanders and Frykberg classification (foot and ankle)
    - By anatomic location
      - Zone I: intermetatarsal and metatarsophalangeal joints
      - Zone II: tarsometatarsal joints
      - Zone III: tarsal joints
      - Zone IV: ankle and subtalar joints
      - Zone V: calcaneus
  - Eichenholtz classification (radiologic)
    - Stage 0: normal
    - Stage I: dislocation, fragmentation, debris; ± osteopenia
    - Stage II: sclerosis, coalescence, resorption of fine debris, ankylosis of large fragments
    - Stage III: develop well-corticated appearance of fragments, ↓ sclerosis, chronic osseous remodeling
- Gross Pathologic & Surgical Features
  - Significant amount of cartilaginous and osseous debris within synovial membrane

CLINICAL ISSUES
- Presentation
  - Most common signs/symptoms
    - Swelling, joint instability/deformity
    - Numbness, paresthesia
  - Clinical profile
    - Diabetes mellitus
      - High prevalence in general population → major cause of Charcot joint, especially in lower limb (ongoing clinical screening and surveillance for neuropathy in foot and ankle is routine)
      - > 10-year history of diabetes mellitus is major risk factor for Charcot joint osteoarthropathy
- Demographics
  - Age
    - Relates to underlying etiology
      - Depends on age of onset of diabetes
      - Congenital pain insensitivity/indifference: teens
  - Epidemiology
    - 15% of diabetic patients develop Charcot joints
    - 20% of syringomyelia patients develop Charcot joints
    - Worldwide; 10-20% of patients with tabes dorsalis develop Charcot joints
- Natural History & Prognosis
  - Progressive joint destruction over time
- Treatment
  - Multidisciplinary approach for long-term care over time; goals include
    - Prevent/control superimposed infection(s)
    - Revascularization if possible, if clinically indicated
    - Manage any associated soft tissue ulcer(s)
    - Preserve quality of life by maintaining mobility and functional independence
    - Amputation in refractory cases, if clinically indicated

DIAGNOSTIC CHECKLIST
- Image Interpretation Pearls
  - Even though debris and other findings may be distant from joint, establish origin from joint-based process to make diagnosis
