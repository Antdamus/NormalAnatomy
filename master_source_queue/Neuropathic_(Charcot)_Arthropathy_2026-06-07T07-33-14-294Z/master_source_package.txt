=== FUSED ARTICLE BACKBONE ===
Terminology and definition:
- Neuropathic/Charcot arthropathy is progressive, often severe joint destruction secondary to long-term polyneuropathy. [Both]
- Etiology is often suggested by location: diabetic foot/ankle and wrist, syringomyelia shoulder and wrist, spinal cord injury/tabes/diabetes spine, alcoholism/tabes hip, and tabes/congenital pain disorders/steroid injection/amyloidosis knee. [Both; STATdx adds amyloidosis, meningomyelocele, and expanded wrist/foot etiologies]

Mechanism:
- Pathogenesis is multifactorial and uncertain, but the useful teaching model combines neurotraumatic and neurovascular mechanisms. [Both]
- Neurotraumatic arm: decreased pain/proprioception leads to laxity, instability, abnormal loading, repetitive microtrauma, fragmentation, and disorganization. [Both]
- Neurovascular arm: abnormal sympathetic/vascular regulation causes hyperemia and an inflammatory osteoclast/osteoblast imbalance, promoting osteolysis and bone resorption. [Both; STATdx phrases this as hyperemia/inflammatory response; RadPrimer emphasizes altered sympathetic control of osseous blood flow]

Imaging pattern:
- Best clue is the Charcot D-pattern: joint dislocation/subluxation/deformity/disorganization, bone debris, cartilage/joint destruction, joint distention/large effusion, and often density/sclerosis or normal bone density for the patient. [Both]
- Radiographs are usually diagnostic and are important for long-term surveillance. [Both]
- CT shows articular destruction and distended joint spaces containing osseous debris; debris may sit several centimeters from the joint because it floats in decompressed effusion, so do not mistake it for tumor matrix. [Both]
- MR is useful for early disease and for problem-solving, especially possible superimposed infection. T1 and postcontrast imaging are more useful than fluid-sensitive sequences for Charcot versus osteomyelitis, though overlap remains substantial. [RadPrimer; STATdx supports MR problem-solving]
- Infection is favored by an ulcer, gas, sinus tract, abscess, discitis-osteomyelitis, or confluent T1 marrow replacement. Hazy/reticular T1 marrow signal and reactive enhancement may be neuropathic rather than infection. [RadPrimer; STATdx supports ulcer/gas/sinus/abscess criteria]

Morphology and staging:
- Hypertrophic form has prominent bony debris, especially knees; atrophic form has resorption with little debris, especially diabetic ankle/foot; combined forms are common. [Both]
- Eichenholtz radiologic staging: stage 0 normal, stage I dislocation/fragmentation/debris +/- osteopenia, stage II sclerosis/coalescence/resorption of fine debris/ankylosis of large fragments, stage III well-corticated fragments with decreased sclerosis and chronic remodeling. [STATdx]
- Sanders-Frykberg foot/ankle zones: I intermetatarsal/MTP, II tarsometatarsal, III tarsal, IV ankle/subtalar, V calcaneus. [STATdx]

Differential diagnosis:
- Osteomyelitis/septic joint can coexist with Charcot; both can have effusions, fluid collections, enhancement, and destruction. Infection clues are ulcer, gas, sinus tract, abscess, and confluent T1 marrow abnormality. [Both]
- Chondrosarcoma can be overcalled in Charcot shoulder because debris in a distended joint may mimic matrix; Charcot debris is osseous and intraarticular, whereas chondrosarcoma matrix is chondroid and the mass is typically extraarticular. [RadPrimer; STATdx supports sarcoma mimic]
- OA/inflammatory/metabolic arthropathy may mimic early or end-stage Charcot, but prominent debris, dislocation, very large effusion, and rapid destruction push toward Charcot. [Both]
- Discitis versus Charcot spine: prominent debris, subluxation, vacuum disc, facet involvement, and risk setting favor Charcot; aspiration may still be needed. [RadPrimer]

Clinical and treatment:
- Presentation is a swollen, unstable or deformed joint with neuropathy; up to 30% may have near-normal proprioception, so careful neurologic exam matters. [RadPrimer]
- Diabetes is common and drives many lower-extremity cases; STATdx notes a >10-year diabetes history as a major risk factor and states that about 15% of diabetic patients develop Charcot joints. [Both/STATdx]
- Treatment is multidisciplinary: control/prevent infection, manage ulcers, consider revascularization when indicated, preserve mobility and independence, and use reconstruction/amputation only when clinically necessary or refractory. [STATdx]

-- Hip Charcot: alcoholism, tabes, rapid destruction, and etiology context --
- STATdx image 13: AP view of the pelvis shows destruction of the left hip <img src="arrow_WC.png">, typical of a Charcot joint, in this case from tabes dorsalis. - STATdx image 14: AP view of the hip in a patient with syphilis shows femoral shaft fracture, dislocation of the femoral head, and joint destruction with scattered debris about the joint. This is typical of a Charcot joint at the hip.
- RadPrimer image 19: AP radiograph in a middle-aged patient gives the initial impression of uncomplicated osteoarthritis, with cartilage loss <img src="arrow_WO.png">, large subchondral cyst formation <img src="arrow_WC.png">, superolateral subluxation of the hip, and calcar buttressing <img src="arrow_WS.png">.

- RadPrimer image 21: AP radiograph in the same patient 7 months later shows the classic hatchet sign <img src="arrow_WS.png"> of rapid destruction of the hip. The rapidity of the destruction and the appearance suggest a diagnosis of rapidly destructive osteoarthritis of the hip. However, one must also consider Charcot arthropathy as an etiology.
- RadPrimer image 22: Axial CT from the same patient shows a small, nodular liver, typical of cirrhosis. The most common etiology of Charcot hip is alcoholism; the cirrhosis proves the etiology in this case.
