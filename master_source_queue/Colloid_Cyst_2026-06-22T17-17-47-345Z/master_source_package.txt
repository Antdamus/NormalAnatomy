Terminology
- [Both] Colloid cyst is a unilocular mucin-containing cyst of the 3rd ventricle, also called a paraphyseal or endodermal cyst.
- [Both] The classic location is the foramen of Monro/anterior-superior roof of the 3rd ventricle, where the pillars of the fornix straddle or drape around the cyst.

Imaging Backbone
- [Both] Best diagnostic clue: a well-demarcated round hyperdense foramen of Monro mass on noncontrast CT.
- [Both] More than 99% are wedged into the foramen of Monro. Rare sites include lateral or 4th ventricles and very rare extraventricular locations such as cerebellar parenchyma, prepontine cistern/meninges, or olfactory groove.
- [Both] Size varies from a few millimeters to several centimeters, with mean around 15 mm. STATdx gives an upper range up to about 4 cm, while RadPrimer lists up to about 3 cm.
- [Both] CT density correlates inversely with hydration state: roughly two-thirds are hyperdense and one-third iso- or hypodense. Hydrocephalus may be present.
- [Both] Contrast enhancement is usually absent. Peripheral/rim enhancement is rare and can be seen in complicated or inflammatory/apoplexy cases.

MR Signal and Modality-Specific Detail
- [Both] T1 signal correlates with cholesterol concentration: about two-thirds are T1 hyperintense and one-third isointense; small isointense cysts may be difficult to see.
- [Both] T2 signal is variable and generally reflects water content; many are isointense to brain, and about 25% show mixed hypo/hyperintensity or a black-hole effect from viscous/inspissated contents.
- [STATdx] Intracystic nodule dot sign may be seen on T2, similar to Rathke cleft cyst; the T2-hypointense nodule likely reflects free radical-containing compounds, supporting an endodermal relationship.
- [Both] FLAIR signal does not suppress. DWI should not show true restricted diffusion; ADC tends to be higher than normal brain and lower than CSF.
- [Both] Fluid-fluid or blood-fluid levels are rare and indicate cyst apoplexy/hemorrhage. Calcification is rare.
- [Both] Recommended imaging includes noncontrast CT and contrast-enhanced MR; serial imaging may be appropriate for asymptomatic cysts under 1 cm without hydrocephalus.

Differential Diagnosis
- [Both] Neurocysticercosis: usually multiple parenchymal/cisternal lesions, often with ependymitis or basilar meningitis, common calcification, and possible scolex.
- [Both] CSF flow artifact / MR pseudocyst: confirm with multiplanar technique and look for phase artifact.
- [Both] Vertebrobasilar dolichoectasia or aneurysm can simulate a hyperdense foramen of Monro mass; look for flow void and phase artifact on MR.
- [Both] Subependymoma favors frontal horn/septum pellucidum attachment and patchy/solid enhancement.
- [Both] Craniopharyngioma rarely involves the 3rd ventricle, is usually not wedged into the foramen of Monro, and more often calcifies or shows rim/nodular enhancement.
- [STATdx] Pituitary adenoma/pituitary neuroendocrine tumor is rare in the 3rd ventricle and usually enhances strongly and uniformly.
- [Both] Choroid plexus papilloma, xanthogranuloma, and choroid plexus cyst are additional intraventricular mimics; xanthogranuloma may be indistinguishable on imaging.

Pathology
- [Both] Colloid cysts arise from embryonic endoderm rather than neuroectoderm, similar to neurenteric and Rathke cleft cysts.
- [Both] Ectopic endodermal elements migrate into the embryonic diencephalic roof; cyst contents accumulate from mucinous secretions and desquamated epithelial cells.
- [Both] Grossly, the lesion is smooth, spherical/ovoid, well delineated, and filled with thick gelatinous contents of variable viscosity; hemorrhage is rare.
- [Both] Histology shows a thin fibrous capsule and simple or pseudostratified epithelial lining with goblet cells and scattered ciliated cells; contents are PAS-positive gelatinous material with variable necrotic debris/cholesterol.
- [STATdx] Cyst contents may include mucin, old blood, and cholesterol.

Clinical Issues and Risk
- [Both] Forty to fifty percent are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally. Headache occurs in about 50-60%; nausea, vomiting, memory loss, altered personality, gait disturbance, and visual symptoms are less common.
- [Both] Acute foramen of Monro obstruction can cause rapid hydrocephalus, herniation, coma, or death, including with small lesions.
- [Both] Peak age is around 40 years. RadPrimer emphasizes the 3rd-4th decades; STATdx broadens the adult range to the 3rd-7th decades. Pediatric cases are rare but may deteriorate precipitously.
- [Both] Colloid cysts account for about 0.5-1.0% of primary brain tumors and 15-20% of intraventricular masses. Familial cases are uncommon but reported; familial cases may be younger and more symptomatic.
- [Both] Most cysts remain stable or stop enlarging; approximately 10% enlarge. Older age, small cyst size, no hydrocephalus, hyperdensity on CT, and T2 hypointensity favor stability; younger age, larger cyst, hydrocephalus, iso-/hypodensity on CT, and T2 hyperintensity favor enlargement.
- [STATdx] Colloid Cyst Risk Score uses age under 65 years, headache, cyst at least 7 mm, FLAIR hyperintensity, and an anterior anatomic risk zone to stratify symptomatic and hydrocephalus risk.

Treatment and Management
- [Both] Complete surgical resection is the usual treatment, commonly neuronavigation-guided endoscopic removal with capsule coagulation.
- [Both] Short-term memory disturbance may occur after surgery and usually resolves. Recurrence is rare after complete resection.
- [Both] Stereotactic aspiration can be difficult when the cyst is extremely viscous or solid. Hyperdensity on CT and T2 hypointensity suggest high viscosity and potential difficulty with percutaneous therapy.
- [Both] Ventricular shunting is an option in selected settings. Observation is uncommon and generally not recommended because sudden obstruction can occur even with small cysts.
- [Both] Referring clinicians should be notified immediately when a colloid cyst is identified, especially if hydrocephalus is present.
