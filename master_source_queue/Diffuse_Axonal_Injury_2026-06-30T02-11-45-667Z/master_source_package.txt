## Fused Teaching Backbone

### Definition And Mechanism

- [Both] Diffuse axonal injury is traumatic diffuse injury to axons in the cerebral hemispheres, corpus callosum, brainstem, and sometimes cerebellum, with concurrent capillary-level vascular injury.
- [Both] The mechanism is sudden acceleration, deceleration, or rotation with abrupt angular momentum change; direct head impact is not required.
- [Both] Axons are stretched more often than literally disconnected at the moment of trauma; later axonal swelling, impaired axoplasmic transport, cytotoxic edema, apoptosis, and Wallerian degeneration account for much of the injury.
- [Both] Injury concentrates where tissues of different density move differently, especially the gray-white junction, corpus callosum, deep gray nuclei/capsules, fornices, corona radiata, brainstem, and cerebellar peduncles.
- [Both] Traumatic microbleeds are an indirect imaging marker of axonal injury because they reflect associated small-vessel damage.

### Imaging Pattern

- [Both] Noncontrast CT is often normal in DAI, with normal initial CT reported in 50-80% of cases; about 75% of clinically suspected DAI with normal CT may have positive MR.
- [Both] CT-visible DAI appears as small hypodense nonhemorrhagic foci or small hyperdense hemorrhagic foci, often punctate to 15 mm and often multiple/bilateral.
- [Both] Typical locations are the frontotemporal gray-white interface, subcortical/deep white matter, corpus callosum, deep gray nuclei, internal/external capsules, fornices, corona radiata, dorsolateral midbrain, and upper pons.
- [Both] Deeper involvement means more severe injury and poorer prognosis; dorsolateral midbrain/upper pontine involvement is especially ominous.
- [Both] FLAIR is key for nonhemorrhagic hyperintense lesions; T2* GRE shows hemorrhagic blooming; SWI depicts substantially more susceptibility foci than GRE; DWI can show restricted diffusion.
- [Both] DTI/tractography can show white-matter tract disruption even when routine MR and GRE are normal; MRS may show reduced NAA/Cr and increased Cho/Cr in normal-appearing or visibly injured brain.
- [RadPrimer] RadPrimer stresses that absent tSAH makes severe DAI unlikely and absent intraventricular hemorrhage makes any DAI less likely.
- [STATdx] STATdx phrases this more cautiously: midline tSAH may suggest DAI, particularly when intraventricular hemorrhage is present.

### Staging And Prognosis

- [Both] Adams/Gennarelli stage 1 involves frontal and temporal gray-white interface lesions; stage 2 adds lobar white matter and corpus callosum; stage 3 includes dorsolateral midbrain and upper pons.
- [STATdx] Extended grading language adds that substantia nigra or mesencephalic tegmentum involvement may further worsen prognosis.
- [Both] Eighty percent of lesions are microscopic and nonhemorrhagic, so visible lesions are the tip of the iceberg.
- [Both] Prognosis worsens with more lesions and deeper lesions; brainstem injury is associated with immediate or early death.

### Differential Diagnosis

- [Both] Multifocal nonhemorrhagic mimics include aging-related leukoaraiosis/lacunes and demyelinating disease.
- [Both] Multifocal hemorrhagic mimics include cerebral amyloid angiopathy, chronic hypertension, cavernous malformations, hemorrhagic tumors, and acute hemorrhagic leukoencephalopathy.
- [Both] Clinical trauma context, lesion distribution at shear interfaces, corpus callosum/brainstem involvement, and the mismatch between poor neurologic status and subtle CT help favor DAI.

### Clinical Context And Management

- [Both] DAI is usually associated with high-velocity motor vehicle trauma but can occur after low-energy trauma such as stair falls or ground-level falls.
- [Both] Suspect DAI when GCS or neurologic impairment is disproportionate to CT findings.
- [Both] Clinical DAI is prolonged posttraumatic coma of at least 6 hours without a mass lesion explaining the coma; severity is graded by coma duration and brainstem deficit.
- [Both] DAI is the most common primary traumatic neuronal injury in the supplied source text and is a major cause of death/disability in children and young adults.
- [Both] There is no lesion-specific curative treatment; management is supportive and treats comorbid herniation, hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, and seizures.

### Diagnostic Checklist

- [Both] In trauma with low GCS and minimal CT findings, obtain MR with FLAIR, DWI, and hemorrhage-sensitive imaging.
- [Both] Use SWI when available for hemorrhagic DAI; use T2* GRE if SWI is unavailable.
- [Both] Search the gray-white junction, corpus callosum (especially splenium/posterior body), deep gray nuclei/capsules, fornices, corona radiata, dorsolateral midbrain, upper pons, and cerebellar peduncles.
- [Both] Report lesion burden, hemorrhagic versus nonhemorrhagic pattern, callosal/brainstem involvement, intraventricular hemorrhage, tSAH, mass lesions, hydrocephalus, and associated traumatic injuries.
