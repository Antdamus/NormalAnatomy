SOURCE-QUALIFIED SYNTHESIS
This fused master source uses RadPrimer as the canonical hierarchy/backbone and STATdx as supplemental depth for image examples, modality-specific details, complications, and recognition reinforcement.

Core teaching frame [Both]
- Scalp and skull trauma should first be sorted into scalp hematoma/laceration, calvarial fracture, skull base fracture, or fracture complication.
- The high-yield distinction is cephalohematoma versus subgaleal hematoma: cephalohematoma is subperiosteal and does not cross sutures, while subgaleal hematoma lies under the galea/aponeurosis, is not suture-limited, may become circumferential, and can be life-threatening in infants.
- Skull fractures are rarely present without overlying scalp swelling; absence of soft-tissue swelling strongly favors normal mimics such as sutures, vascular grooves, venous lakes, arachnoid granulations, or wormian bones.

Terminology [Both]
- Scalp injuries include lacerations and hematomas; lacerations may contain discontinuity, air, or foreign material.
- Skull injuries are fractures. Linear fractures are sharply marginated; depressed fractures implode fragments inward; elevated fractures rotate/lift a segment; diastatic fractures widen a suture or synchondrosis; growing skull fractures reflect posttraumatic leptomeningeal cyst formation.

Imaging approach [RadPrimer backbone, STATdx supplemental examples]
- CT is the practical modality for acute head trauma and skull fracture detection. Radiographs have no modern role in routine head trauma evaluation.
- Cephalohematoma appears as a unilateral scalp mass limited by sutures; chronic lesions can calcify.
- Subgaleal hematoma appears as extensive soft-tissue swelling that can cross sutures, cross midline, and extend around the calvarium.
- For skull base fractures involving the temporal bone, sphenoid/clivus, carotid canal, jugular foramen, or dural venous sinus, consider CTA/MRA because vascular or venous sinus injury risk is higher.
- MR is mainly for complications rather than initial acute fracture detection, but it can show complications such as growing skull fracture/leptomeningeal cyst.

Clinical and prognosis points [Both]
- Cephalohematoma occurs in about 1% of newborns, is often related to instrumented or vacuum-assisted delivery, is usually diagnosed clinically, and generally resolves without treatment.
- Subgaleal hematoma can occur at any age after trauma and can be life-threatening in infants if large or expanding.
- Linear fractures tend to follow lower-impact force over a broad surface; depressed fractures reflect higher-energy direct impact over a smaller surface.
- Complications to actively look for include vascular injury, dural/arachnoid laceration with possible CSF leak, cranial nerve injury, and rare growing skull fracture.
