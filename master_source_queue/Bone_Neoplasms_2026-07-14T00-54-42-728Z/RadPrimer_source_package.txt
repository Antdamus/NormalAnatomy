TITLE: Bone Neoplasms

KEY FACTS
- Imaging
  - Whole-body survey shows increased activity in primary malignant bone tumor and metastases in osteosarcoma, chondrosarcoma, and Ewing sarcoma
  - Radiographic skeletal survey is more useful than bone scan in multiple myeloma secondary to lytic nature and often indolent disease
  - Benign bone lesions mostly have variable appearance on bone scan and should always be correlated with other imaging
    - Primary diagnosis of benign bone tumor is by radiograph, CT, and MR
    - Bone scan can evaluate for multifocal disease and further evaluate incidental sclerotic lesions on CT or radiographs
  - Stage and assess response to therapy in malignant bone tumors with whole-body F-18 FDG PET/CT
  - Large variances of uptake in benign bone tumors limit use of F-18 FDG PET/CT in clinical practice in evaluating benign bone tumors
- Clinical Issues
  - Osteochondroma is most common benign bone tumor
  - Osteosarcoma is most common primary bone malignancy (excluding multiple myeloma), ~ 30% of all primary bone malignancies
- Diagnostic Checklist
  - Tc-99m MDP bone scan useful to identify polyostotic or metastatic lesions in osteosarcoma, chondrosarcoma, and Ewing sarcoma
  - Increased uptake between ribs on bone scan is concerning for osteosarcoma lung metastases
  - Heterotopic ossification after surgery can be difficult to distinguish on bone scan from tumor recurrence
  - Postsurgical change can show increased bone uptake on bone scan
  - New uptake in area of benign lesions could represent fracture or malignant transformation

TERMINOLOGY
- Definitions
  - Malignant primary bone lesion
    - Osteosarcoma: Malignant cells of mesenchymal origin that produce osteoid
    - Ewing sarcoma: Primitive undifferentiated neoplasm; small, blue, round cell tumor
    - Chondrosarcoma: Heterogeneous bone tumors; common trait of chondroid matrix production
    - Multiple myeloma: Neoplastic proliferation of plasma cells in bone marrow
  - Polyostotic: Affects multiple bones
    - Skeletal scintigraphy plays large role in polyostotic bone lesions to accurately assess extent of disease
  - Monostotic: Affects single bone
  - Differentiating monostotic from polyostotic is important to narrow differential diagnosis
  - Not all bone lesions fall into 1 category; can have variable presentation
- Bone Imaging and Dysplasias
  - Malignant
    - Hot: Chondrosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma, osteosarcoma, metastases, adamantinoma
    - Cold: Multiple myeloma; metastases, purely lytic
  - Benign
    - Hot: Paget disease, osteoblastoma, chondroblastoma, aneurysmal bone cyst, osteoid osteoma, giant cell tumor, fibrous dysplasia
    - Isointense or mild: Fibrous cortical defect, nonossifying fibroma, enchondroma
    - Cold or normal uptake: Enostosis, osteopathia striata, bone cyst
    - Variable: Osteochondroma/hereditary multiple exostoses, Langerhans cell histiocytosis, hemangioma

IMAGING
- General Features
  - Best diagnostic clue
    - Tc-99m MDP bone scan
      - Whole-body survey shows increased activity in primary malignant bone tumor and metastases in osteosarcoma, chondrosarcoma, and Ewing sarcoma
      - Multiple myeloma: Radiographic skeletal survey shows lytic lesions; bone scan not useful due to lytic nature and often indolent disease
      - Benign bone lesions mostly have variable appearance on bone scan
        - Primary diagnosis of benign bone tumor is by radiograph, CT, and MR
        - Utility of bone scan is to evaluate for multifocal disease or evaluate incidental sclerotic lesions on CT or radiographs
      - 3-phase bone scan can also be performed, may be useful in diagnosis
    - F-18 FDG PET/CT
      - Primary malignant osseous tumors (osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma, and chondrosarcoma) show intense uptake of tracer
        - Stage and assess response to therapy
      - Multiple myeloma: Increased activity in lytic bone lesion or extramedullary site
      - Large variances of uptake in benign bone tumors limit use of F-18 FDG PET/CT in clinical practice in evaluating benign bone tumors
  - Morphology
    - Benign
      - Usually solitary
      - If multiple, consider Ollier disease, Maffucci syndrome
  - Location and skeletal scintigraphic appearance
    - Osteosarcoma
      - Typically long bone metaphyses, particularly distal femur; also proximal tibia and humerus
      - Axial involvement: Adults > children
      - Intense, often heterogeneous uptake on bone scan
    - Ewing sarcoma
      - Most often in extremity long bones, particularly femur, and also pelvic bones
      - Less common: Spine, hands, and feet
      - Intense, more homogeneous uptake on bone scan
    - Chondrosarcoma
      - Most frequent malignant bone tumor after multiple myeloma and osteosarcoma
      - Incidence of axial and appendicular involvement is very similar
      - Bone scan uptake is usually intense
      - Primary: Arise de novo
      - Secondary: Arise in benign precursor, either osteochondroma or enchondroma
      - Anatomic classification: Intramedullary (central), peripheral, juxtacortical (periosteal)
      - Histologic classification: Conventional (~ 85%), dedifferentiated, mesenchymal, clear cell
      - Central chondrosarcomas (~ 75%)
        - Arise within medullary cavity
        - Most common: Proximal femur, pelvic bones, proximal humerus
        - Precursor lesion: Enchondroma (up to 40%)
      - Peripheral chondrosarcomas (~ 10%)
        - Arise from cartilage cap of preexisting osteochondroma
        - Most common: Pelvic bones and shoulder girdle
        - Precursor lesion: Osteochondroma
      - Dedifferentiated (~ 10%)
        - Common in pelvis, femur, proximal humerus, ribs
        - Can be intraosseous or extraosseous mass
        - Precursor lesion: Rarely in multiple osteochondromas and enchondromatosis
      - Clear cell (< 2%)
        - Epiphysis of long bones
      - Mesenchymal (< 2%)
        - Maxilla, mandible common
      - Periosteal chondrosarcomas (< 1%)
        - Very rare tumor that arises on surface of bones
        - Long bones most common site, particularly distal femur
    - Multiple myeloma
      - Plasma cells in bone marrow
      - Lytic lesions throughout skeleton
      - No role for bone scan
    - Adamantinoma
      - Common site: Diaphysis of long bones, especially tibia
      - Low-grade malignant lesion with focal high uptake
    - Osteoid osteoma
      - Cortically based lesion
      - Can be in any bone; common in long bones
      - 3-phase positive with double density sign, intense central uptake, and moderate surrounding uptake
      - Delayed uptake in nidus secondary to presence of osteoblasts
    - Langerhans cell histiocytosis
      - Commonly affected bone is skull followed by upper limb long bones and flat bones
      - Solitary more common than multiple
      - Generally whole-body radiologic skeletal survey preferred over bone scan
      - Bone scan useful to determine active lesions and response to therapy
    - Fibrous dysplasia
      - Monostotic (affecting 1 bone) and polyostotic (affecting > 1 bone)
      - Common sites for monostotic type: Ribs, proximal femur, and craniofacial bones, typically posterior maxilla
      - Bone scan is useful to identify and determine extent of bone lesions
    - Giant cell tumors
      - Common location: Epiphysis of long bones
      - Distal femur, proximal tibia, distal radius, sacrum, and proximal humerus
      - Bone scan: Diffuse increased or peripheral tracer uptake with photopenia centrally (doughnut sign)
    - Aneurysmal bone cyst
      - Common location: Around knee, pelvis, or spine
      - Can show diffuse uptake or peripheral uptake and central photopenia
    - Hemangioma
      - Common sites: Vertebral bodies and craniofacial bones
      - Variable uptake on bone scan: Normal/warm/cold
    - Unicameral bone cyst
      - Majority in proximal humerus and proximal femur
      - Can be cold defect or increased peripheral uptake and absent central activity
      - Fracture complication would show increased uptake
    - Osteoblastoma
      - Common sites: Posterior elements of spine; sacrum and long bones
      - Focal increased uptake, nonspecific
    - Enchondroma
      - Common site: Miniature long bones of hands and feet
      - Also can involve femur, tibia, humerus
      - Typically solitary lesions
      - Multiple lesions (Ollier disease and Maffucci syndrome)
      - Normal or mildly increased uptake on bone scan
      - Fractures can show change in uptake when following these lesions
      - Bone scan cannot reliably differentiate low-grade chondrosarcoma from enchondroma
    - Fibrous cortical defect (FCD)/nonossifying fibroma (NOF)
      - Common location: Metaphysis of femur, tibia
      - NOF is much larger than FCD
      - Variable degrees of focal increased tracer uptake on bone scan depending on level of ossification
        - No or faint uptake in "healed" lesions filled with bone, mild to moderate uptake in "healing" lesions that are filling with normal bone
    - Enostosis (bone island)/osteopoikilosis
      - Essentially, intramedullary displacement of compact lamellar bone
      - Can occur anywhere; more often in pelvis and proximal femur
      - Osteopoikilosis: Autosomal dominant condition with multiple enostoses
      - Usually normal bone scan; larger lesions may show very mild uptake
    - Osteopathia striata
      - Dense linear striations in diaphyses and metaphyses of long and tubular bones
      - Parallel striations to long axis of bones
      - Iliac bones may show fan-shaped striations
      - No significant uptake on bone scan
    - Chondroblastoma
      - Well-defined, osteolytic lesion located in epiphyses or apophyses of long bones
      - Commonly affected site is femur, followed by humerus, tibia
      - Patella and tarsal bones
      - Skeletally immature patient
      - Bone scan shows focal increased uptake
    - Osteochondroma
      - Most common benign bone tumor
      - Commonly in femur, tibia (around knees), and humerus
      - Metaphyseal location with cortical and medullary bone continuous with underlying bone
      - Majority of these tumors are solitary nonhereditary lesions
      - Hereditary multiple exostoses are characterized by development of multiple osteochondromas
      - Uptake on bone scan is directly correlated with degree of endochondral ossification
- Nuclear Medicine Findings
  - Tc-99m MDP bone scan
    - Variable uptake on bone scan in benign lesions
    - Fracture, sarcomatous degeneration: Tend to have increased bone scan activity, hyperemia on vascular phase
    - Assess for multiple lesions on whole-body scan
    - Malignant primary bone lesions (except multiple myeloma) have increased uptake
- Imaging Recommendations
  - Best imaging tool
    - Plain film and CT (benign)
      - Chondroid matrix in medullary-centered lytic regions
      - Pathologic fractures
      - Aggressive characteristics worrisome for sarcoma
      - Lytic-appearing areas in medullary space, ± chondroid calcifications
  - Protocol advice
    - Additional nuclear medicine imaging options
      - F-18 FDG PET/CT: Focal hypermetabolic activity worrisome for sarcomatous degeneration if fracture excluded
        - However, benign enchondroma can show increased FDG
  - MR
    - Lobulated borders
    - May have increased T2 signal due to water in hyaline cartilage

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS
- Multiple Myeloma
  - Incidence slightly higher in men
  - Median age is 70 years
- Osteosarcoma
  - Most common primary bone malignancy excluding multiple myeloma, 35% of all primary bone malignancies
  - Age: 75% < 20 years, 15-20% > 65 years
  - M > F, slight difference
  - African American higher incidence than Caucasian
- Chondrosarcoma
  - Can occur from sarcomatous transformation of benign lesions (enchondroma, osteochondroma)
  - Accounts for 26% of primary bone malignancies
  - Age
    - Low incidence in children
    - Central: Peak 5th-7th, secondary 4th-5th decades
    - Peripheral: Younger than central
    - Periosteal: 80% > 20 years
    - Mesenchymal: 80% 1st-4th decades
    - Clear cell: 3rd-4th decade
    - Differentiated: Most > 5th decade
- Ewing Sarcoma
  - Overall, accounts for 16% of all primary bone malignancies
  - Age: Typically 5-20 years
  - M:F = 1.5:1

PATHOLOGY
- General Features
  - Associated abnormalities
    - Benign
      - Risk of sarcomatous degeneration
      - Pathologic fracture
- Staging, Grading, & Classification
  - AJCC staging for cancers of appendicular skeleton, trunk, skull, and facial bones
    - IA: T1 G1/GX; IB: T2-3 G1/GX
    - IIA: T1 G2/G3; IIB: T2 G2/G3
    - III: T3 G2/G3
    - IVA: M1a; IVB: M1b or N1M1a/b
      - TX: Primary tumor cannot be assessed
      - T0: No evidence of primary tumor
      - T1: Tumor ≤ 8 cm in greatest dimension
      - T2: Tumor > 8 cm in greatest dimension
      - T3: Discontinuous tumors in primary bone site
      - N1: Regional nodal metastases
      - M1a: Lung metastasis; M1b: Secondary bone or other distant sites including distant lymph nodes
      - GX: Grade cannot be assessed
      - G1: Well differentiated, low grade
      - G2: Moderately differentiated, high grade
      - G3: Poorly differentiated, high grade
  - Musculoskeletal Tumor Society Staging System for malignant bone tumors
    - Stage I: Low-grade, localized tumors
    - Stage II: High-grade, localized tumors
      - A or B designation for inside vs. outside bone, respectively
    - Stage III: Metastatic tumors
  - Multiple myeloma (International Staging System)
    - Stage I: Serum β-2-microglobulin < 3.5 mg/L and albumin level ≥ 3.5 g/dL and no high-risk cytogenetics
    - Stage II: β-2-microglobulin level 3.5-5.5 or albumin < 3.5 and β-2-microglobulin < 3.5
    - Stage III: Serum β-2-microglobulin ≥ 5.5 and high-risk cytogenetics &/or high LDH levels
- Gross Pathologic & Surgical Features
  - Benign
    - Ectopic hyaline cartilage rests in intramedullary bone
    - Replaced trabeculae with mineralized, unmineralized cartilage

CLINICAL ISSUES
- Presentation
  - Most common signs/symptoms
    - Bone pain; often worse at night
    - Soft tissue swelling or mass; tenderness or warmth in affected area
    - Pathologic fracture
    - Benign
      - Pain (± pathologic fracture)
      - Often asymptomatic
  - Other signs/symptoms
    - Benign: Growth through cortex + soft tissue mass highly suggestive of sarcomatous transformation
- Demographics
  - Multiple myeloma
    - More common than other primary bone malignancies
    - Incidence slightly higher in men
    - Median age is 70 years
  - Osteosarcoma
    - Most common primary bone malignancy (excluding multiple myeloma), ~ 30% of all primary bone malignancies
    - Age: 75% < 20 years, 15-20% > 65 years
    - M > F, slight difference
    - African American higher incidence than Caucasian
  - Chondrosarcoma
    - Can occur from sarcomatous transformation of benign lesions (enchondroma, osteochondroma)
    - Accounts for 26% of primary bone malignancies
    - Age
      - Low incidence in children
      - Central: Peak 5th-7th, secondary 4th-5th decades
      - Peripheral: Younger than central
      - Periosteal: 80% > 20 years
      - Mesenchymal: 80% 1st-4th decades
      - Clear cell: 3rd-4th decade
      - Differentiated: Most > 5th decade
  - Ewing sarcoma
    - Overall, accounts for 16% of all primary bone malignancies
    - Most common primary bone malignancy in children
    - Age range is typically 5-20 years
    - M:F = 1.5:1

DIAGNOSTIC CHECKLIST
- Consider
  - Tc-99m MDP bone scan
    - Identify metastatic lesions in osteosarcoma, chondrosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma
    - Not useful for tumor margins or soft tissue component
    - Not useful for multiple myeloma
    - Can evaluate bone pain with SPECT/CT if planar bone scan normal to increase sensitivity
  - F-18 FDG PET/CT
    - Useful for staging primary bone malignancies
    - Degree of uptake often corresponds with tumor grade in chondrosarcoma
    - Has been shown to predict therapeutic response in osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma
    - Full inspiration CT has better sensitivity for small lung nodules than F-18 FDG PET/CT
    - Increased activity in lytic lesion or extramedullary sites of multiple myeloma
- Image Interpretation Pearls
  - Increased uptake between ribs on bone scan is concerning for osteosarcoma lung metastases
    - Oblique and lateral views help differentiate soft tissue vs. bone lesions
  - Postsurgical change can show increased uptake
  - Heterotopic ossification after surgery can be difficult to distinguish on bone scan from tumor recurrence
  - Benign bone lesions mostly have variable appearance on bone scan
    - Bone scan can evaluate for multifocal disease
    - New uptake in area of benign lesions could represent fracture or malignant transformation
    - CT or radiographs can help to distinguish
