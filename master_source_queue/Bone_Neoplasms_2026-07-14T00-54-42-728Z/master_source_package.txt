## Fused Teaching Backbone

### Terminology And Scope

- [Both] Bone neoplasms include malignant primary bone lesions such as osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma, chondrosarcoma, and multiple myeloma, plus benign tumors and tumor-like lesions whose scintigraphic activity varies widely.
- [Both] Osteosarcoma is a malignant mesenchymal tumor that produces osteoid; Ewing sarcoma is a primitive small round blue cell tumor; chondrosarcoma is defined by chondroid matrix production; multiple myeloma is plasma-cell neoplasia in marrow.
- [Both] Polyostotic disease affects multiple bones and is a major reason to use skeletal scintigraphy; monostotic disease affects one bone and usually has a narrower differential.
- [Both] Not every lesion fits neatly into hot/cold or benign/malignant categories, so nuclear medicine findings must be reconciled with radiographs, CT, and MR.

### Bone Scan And PET Pattern Language

- [Both] Osteosarcoma, chondrosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma, blastic metastases, and adamantinoma are typically hot on Tc-99m MDP bone scan.
- [Both] Multiple myeloma and purely lytic metastases may be cold or poorly seen on bone scan; radiographic skeletal survey and cross-sectional imaging are more useful for myeloma because of its lytic, often indolent behavior.
- [Both] Benign hot lesions include Paget disease, osteoblastoma, chondroblastoma, aneurysmal bone cyst, osteoid osteoma, giant cell tumor, and fibrous dysplasia.
- [Both] Enchondroma and nonossifying fibroma may be isointense or mildly avid; enostosis, osteopathia striata, and simple bone cyst may be cold or normal; osteochondroma, hereditary multiple exostoses, Langerhans cell histiocytosis, and hemangioma are variable.
- [STATdx] STATdx explicitly frames osteochondroma as an exostosis with a cartilaginous component and notes FDG PET/CT is more useful than bone scan for active Langerhans cell histiocytosis assessment and therapy response.

### Imaging Strategy

- [Both] Whole-body Tc-99m MDP bone scan surveys primary malignant bone tumors and metastases in osteosarcoma, chondrosarcoma, and Ewing sarcoma, and it is useful for detecting multifocal or polyostotic disease.
- [Both] Bone scan is not useful for defining tumor margins or soft-tissue components; plain radiographs, CT, and MR remain essential for primary diagnosis and local anatomic characterization.
- [Both] Benign lesions should be correlated with radiograph/CT/MR because benign uptake ranges from absent to intense, and new uptake may reflect fracture, healing, or malignant transformation.
- [Both] F-18 FDG PET/CT is useful for staging malignant bone tumors and therapy-response assessment; uptake can correlate with grade in chondrosarcoma and can predict response in osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma.
- [Both] Full-inspiration chest CT is more sensitive than FDG PET/CT for small lung nodules, so suspected pulmonary metastatic disease still needs diagnostic CT technique.

### Tumor-Specific Patterns

- [Both] Osteosarcoma most often involves long-bone metaphyses, especially distal femur, proximal tibia, and proximal humerus; adults have more axial involvement than children; bone scan uptake is often intense and heterogeneous.
- [Both] Ewing sarcoma most often involves long bones, especially femur, and pelvic bones; uptake is usually intense and more homogeneous.
- [Both] Chondrosarcoma has similar axial and appendicular distribution and usually intense uptake. Central tumors arise in medullary cavity, often proximal femur, pelvis, or proximal humerus, and may arise from enchondroma. Peripheral tumors arise from an osteochondroma cartilage cap, often pelvis or shoulder girdle.
- [Both] Multiple myeloma produces lytic skeletal lesions and is usually better assessed with skeletal survey/cross-sectional imaging than bone scan; FDG PET/CT can show active lytic or extramedullary disease and treatment response.
- [Both] Osteoid osteoma is cortically based, often in long bones, and can show a three-phase positive double-density sign with intense nidus uptake and moderate surrounding activity.
- [Both] Fibrous dysplasia may be monostotic or polyostotic; bone scan is useful for lesion extent. Giant cell tumor can show diffuse or peripheral uptake with central photopenia. Aneurysmal bone cyst can also show peripheral uptake with central photopenia.
- [Both] Osteochondroma uptake correlates with endochondral ossification activity; cortical and medullary continuity with parent bone is the key anatomic feature.
- [Both] Enchondroma may show normal or mild uptake and bone scan cannot reliably separate enchondroma from low-grade chondrosarcoma.

### Differential And Demographics

- [Both] Multiple myeloma is more common than the other primary bone malignancies, has slightly higher incidence in men, and has median age around 70 years.
- [Both] Osteosarcoma is the most common primary bone malignancy when multiple myeloma is excluded, about 30-35% of primary bone malignancies; most patients are younger than 20 years, with a smaller older-adult peak.
- [STATdx] STATdx uses current race wording, noting higher incidence in Black patients than White patients.
- [Both] Chondrosarcoma accounts for about 26% of primary bone malignancies and may arise from sarcomatous transformation of enchondroma or osteochondroma; central tumors peak in older adults, while peripheral tumors skew younger than central tumors.
- [Both] Ewing sarcoma accounts for about 16% of primary bone malignancies, usually affects patients 5-20 years old, and is the most common primary bone malignancy in children in the supplied text.

### Staging, Classification, And Management-Relevant Details

- [Both] AJCC 8th edition staging for appendicular skeleton, trunk, skull, and facial bone cancers uses size threshold 8 cm, discontinuous tumors in the primary bone site, nodal disease, lung metastasis, other distant metastasis, and grade.
- [Both] Musculoskeletal Tumor Society/Enneking staging separates low-grade localized, high-grade localized, and metastatic tumors, with A/B describing intracompartmental versus extracompartmental extension.
- [Both] Multiple myeloma International Staging System uses serum beta-2-microglobulin, albumin, cytogenetics, and LDH.
- [Both] Symptoms include bone pain, often worse at night, soft-tissue swelling or mass, tenderness/warmth, and pathologic fracture; benign lesions may be painful or asymptomatic.
- [Both] Growth through cortex with soft-tissue mass in a benign lesion is highly concerning for sarcomatous transformation.

### Diagnostic Checklist

- [Both] Use Tc-99m MDP bone scan to identify metastatic or polyostotic lesions in osteosarcoma, chondrosarcoma, and Ewing sarcoma.
- [Both] Do not rely on bone scan for tumor margins, soft-tissue components, or multiple myeloma burden.
- [Both] Use SPECT/CT when planar bone scan is normal but focal bone pain persists and higher sensitivity/localization is needed.
- [Both] Consider FDG PET/CT for staging malignant primary bone tumors, response assessment, active myeloma lesions, and suspected sarcomatous degeneration after excluding fracture.
- [Both] Foci of intrathoracic uptake, especially between ribs or outside the expected rib contour, are concerning for osteosarcoma pulmonary metastases; oblique/lateral views help localize bone versus soft tissue.
- [Both] Postsurgical uptake and heterotopic ossification can mimic recurrence; CT or radiographs help distinguish postoperative change, fracture, benign lesion activity, and malignant transformation.
