=== FUSED ARTICLE BACKBONE ===

KEY FACTS
- [Both] Temporal bone fracture evaluation is primarily high-resolution bone algorithm MDCT with coronal reconstructions; CTA/MRA is considered when the fracture reaches or threatens the carotid canal, and CTV/MRV is considered for dural venous sinus or jugular foramen involvement.
- [Both] Dedicated brain imaging is critical because intracranial injury is common, reported in up to 90% of patients with temporal bone fractures.
- [Both] Longitudinal fractures parallel the long axis of the petrous ridge and commonly involve the external auditory canal, middle ear, and ossicular chain; otic capsule involvement is uncommon.
- [Both] Transverse fractures are perpendicular to the petrous ridge long axis and more often violate the otic capsule, internal auditory canal, labyrinth, and facial nerve canal.
- [Both] Oblique fractures have mixed features and are common in practice, so the older longitudinal/transverse scheme is less predictive than otic-capsule violating versus otic-capsule sparing classification.
- [Both] Otic-capsule violation better predicts sensorineural hearing loss, facial nerve injury, CSF leak, perilymph fistula, and intracranial complications.
- [Both] Ossicular injury is usually dislocation rather than fracture; the incus is most often involved, with incudostapedial and incudomalleolar disruptions common.
- [Both] Always assess the facial nerve canal, otic capsule, tegmen, carotid canal, ossicles, central skull base extension, intracranial injuries, and cervical spine injuries.

TERMINOLOGY
- [Both] Temporal bone fracture is traumatic injury of the temporal bone with or without ossicular injury.
- [Both] Otic-capsule sparing fractures form the majority and often extend through the temporal squamosa, external auditory canal, tympanic membrane, middle ear, mastoid, and tegmen.
- [Both] Otic-capsule violating fractures are less common, roughly 5-20%, but have higher rates of sensorineural hearing loss, CNVII injury, CSF leak, pneumolabyrinth, and labyrinthine complications.

IMAGING
- [Both] Best diagnostic clue: bone CT shows a fracture line. Secondary signs include hemotympanum, mastoid or middle ear opacification, pneumolabyrinth, intracranial air/hemorrhage, or unexplained parapharyngeal air suggesting an occult mastoid fracture.
- [Both] Otic-capsule sparing fractures commonly involve the external auditory canal, tympanic membrane, middle ear, and ossicles; CNVII canal involvement, when present, often affects the geniculate or tympanic segments.
- [Both] Otic-capsule violating fractures may extend from occipital impact regions through the petrous pyramid, cochlea, vestibule, semicircular canals, IAC, jugular foramen, foramen lacerum, or central skull base.
- [STATdx] STATdx adds emphasis that labyrinthine structures are more often affected in otic-capsule violating fractures than otic-capsule sparing fractures.
- [Both] Perilymph fistula reflects oval or round window rupture with communication between middle ear and membranous labyrinth; subtle CT signs include pneumolabyrinth and fluid at the oval or round window.
- [Both] CTA/MRA is considered when fracture involves the carotid canal; carotid canal fracture is only moderately associated with ICA injury, but vascular injury can include dissection, occlusion, or carotid-cavernous fistula.
- [Both] CTV/MRV is considered when fracture involves a dural venous sinus, sigmoid sinus, jugular bulb, or jugular foramen region.
- [Both] MRI can show hemotympanum/hemolabyrinth, loss of labyrinthine T2 signal from hemolabyrinth/pneumolabyrinth/posttraumatic labyrinthitis ossificans, CNVII or membranous labyrinth enhancement, meningitis/abscess, vascular dissection/occlusion, or venous thrombosis.

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS
- [Both] Pseudofractures include sutures, fissures, canaliculi, and aqueducts; they are typically sclerotic, corticated, bilateral, and symmetric. Compare with the contralateral side.
- [Both] External and skull-base fissures/sutures that can mimic fracture include temporoparietal, petrooccipital, sphenopetrosal, occipitomastoid, petrotympanic, petrosquamosal, tympanosquamous, and tympanomastoid lines.
- [Both] Canaliculi and aqueducts that can mimic fracture include mastoid, inferior tympanic, subarcuate/petromastoid, singular canaliculi, cochlear aqueduct, and vestibular aqueduct.
- [Both] Incus interposition surgery can mimic chronic incus dislocation.
- [Both] Mastoiditis or preexisting mastoid/middle-ear effusion can mimic hemotympanum or CSF leak; correlate with trauma signs and fracture line.

PATHOLOGY AND MECHANISM
- [Both] Temporal bone fracture requires high-force impact, most commonly motor vehicle accident; longitudinal fractures tend to follow lateral impact, transverse fractures occipital or frontal impact, and tympanic plate fractures a blow to the chin.
- [STATdx] Penetrating injury may disrupt the ossicular chain, as illustrated by STATdx image 4.

CLINICAL ISSUES
- [Both] Common signs include Battle sign, EAC hemorrhage, hemotympanum, conductive hearing loss, sensorineural hearing loss, facial nerve dysfunction, CSF leak, vertigo, and perilymphatic fistula symptoms.
- [Both] Conductive hearing loss early after trauma may reflect hemotympanum or tympanic membrane injury; persistent conductive hearing loss should trigger careful evaluation for ossicular disruption.
- [Both] Sensorineural hearing loss may reflect otic capsule, IAC, brainstem, perilymph fistula, hemolabyrinth, or labyrinthine concussion injury.
- [Both] Delayed facial paresis often reflects reversible injury and is usually managed conservatively; immediate complete paralysis has worse prognosis and may lead to surgical decompression or repair depending on clinical context.
- [Both] Most CSF leaks resolve spontaneously within 7 days; persistent leak may need surgery. Meningitis occurs in a minority of patients with CSF leak.
- [Both] Chronic complications include acquired cephalocele, acquired cholesteatoma from squamous invasion of the fracture site, and external auditory canal stenosis.
- [Both] Management prioritizes severe head injury; anticoagulation or endovascular therapy may be used for carotid injury, antibiotics may be used when CSF leak is present, and CNVII management remains controversial.

DIAGNOSTIC CHECKLIST
- [Both] Use a fixed search pattern: fracture orientation and otic capsule status, CNVII canal, cochlea/vestibule/SCCs/IAC, ossicles, tegmen, EAC/TMJ, carotid canal, jugular foramen/sigmoid sinus, central skull base, intracranial compartment, and cervical spine.
- [Both] Classify using both anatomic direction and otic-capsule status in reports.
- [Both] Do not overcall pseudofracture; look for corticated margins, symmetry, and known normal fissures/canaliculi/aqueducts.
