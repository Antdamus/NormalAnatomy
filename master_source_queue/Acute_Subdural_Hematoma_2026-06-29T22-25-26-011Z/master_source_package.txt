## Fused Teaching Backbone

### Definition And Mechanism

- [Both] Acute subdural hematoma is acute blood in or between the inner border cell layer of the dura and the arachnoid.
- [Both] Trauma is the most common cause; the classic mechanism is tearing of bridging cortical veins as they cross the subdural space to drain into the dural sinuses.
- [Both] Older adults are especially vulnerable after minor trauma because cerebral atrophy permits greater cortical motion and bridging-vein strain.
- [STATdx] Nontraumatic/spontaneous causes include dissection of intraparenchymal hemorrhage into the subarachnoid/subdural space, aneurysm rupture, dural AVF/AVM/cavernoma, moyamoya, dural tumor invasion, severe coagulopathy, and cerebral amyloid angiopathy.
- [Both] More than 70% of acute SDH cases have other significant traumatic lesions; if mass effect or shift is greater than expected for hematoma thickness, suspect underlying edema/excitotoxic injury.

### Imaging Pattern

- [Both] Noncontrast CT is the initial screening study. Use bone and soft-tissue algorithms, standard brain windows, and wider windows around 150 HU when SDH is subtle or isodense.
- [Both] The typical CT finding is a crescentic hyperdense extraaxial collection that spreads diffusely over the affected convexity and may extend along the falx, tentorium, and anterior/middle cranial fossa floors.
- [Both] Acute SDH may cross sutures but does not cross dural attachments. It causes inward displacement of cortical veins, sulci, and the gray-white interface.
- [Both] Hyperacute SDH can be heterogeneous or hypodense. In the 6-hour to 3-day acute window, about 60% are homogeneously hyperdense and about 40% are mixed density due to active bleeding/swirl, CSF admixture from arachnoid tear, or clot retraction.
- [STATdx] STATdx explicitly frames CSF admixture as hematohygroma/arachnoid tear physiology; this is useful when an acute SDH has unexpectedly low-density components.
- [Both] Rare isodense acute SDH occurs with coagulopathy or severe anemia and is best detected by gray-white/cortical vein displacement and widened window settings.
- [Both] Coronal and sagittal reformats are best for small falcine, tentorial, or convexity components that are difficult on axial images alone.
- [Both] MR is more sensitive than CT but is rarely the initial trauma study; it better depicts extent, age variability, traumatic brain injury, and nontraumatic causes.

### MR And CTA Details

- [Both] Acute SDH signal is variable and can evolve like intraparenchymal hemorrhage, but recurrent bleeding and CSF dilution make exact age assignment difficult.
- [Both] On T1WI, hyperacute SDH is iso- to mildly hyperintense and acute SDH may be mildly hypointense; on T2WI, hyperacute SDH may be mildly hyperintense and acute SDH is often hypointense.
- [Both] FLAIR is often conspicuous because SDH is usually hyperintense to CSF, though acute collections can be isointense to CSF from T2-shortening effects.
- [Both] T2* GRE is usually hypointense unless the collection is hyperacute; DWI is nonspecific but helps distinguish empyema when central diffusion restriction is marked.
- [Both] CTA mainly demonstrates mass effect and displaced veins; DSA is reserved when CTA suggests aneurysm or dural vascular malformation.
- [STATdx] STATdx images add a CTA spot-sign/active extravasation teaching cluster: STATdx image 8 follows the heterogeneous SDH in STATdx image 7, and STATdx image 9 shows the 24-hour enlargement before decompression.

### Differential Diagnosis

- [Both] Other subdural collections include mixed acute-on-chronic/subacute SDH, hygroma, effusion, and empyema.
- [Both] Hygroma is clear CSF from torn arachnoid, often after surgery or trauma; effusion is near-CSF-density xanthochromic fluid from membrane/plasma extravasation or meningitis.
- [Both] Subdural empyema shows peripheral enhancement, FLAIR hyperintensity, and restricted diffusion on DWI.
- [Both] Acute epidural hematoma is typically biconvex, strongly fracture-associated, limited by sutures, and may cross dural attachments; acute SDH is usually crescentic and can cross sutures.
- [Both] Pachymeningopathies, dural tumors, and chemical shift artifact can mimic subdural pathology in the right setting.
- [STATdx] Skull metastases may result in acute SDH, so bone windows should be reviewed when the context is atypical or nontraumatic.

### Clinical Course And Management

- [Both] Presentation ranges from asymptomatic to loss of consciousness. A lucid interval can occur when an initially alert patient deteriorates hours after trauma.
- [Both] Advanced age, early symptoms within 4 hours, anticoagulation, and coagulopathy increase risk and worsen prognosis.
- [Both] Acute SDH can grow with increasing mass effect if untreated. Recurrent hemorrhage is common; in children, recurrent or mixed-age hemorrhage should raise concern for nonaccidental trauma.
- [Both] Thin isolated falcotentorial acute SDH, around 3 mm or less, in a young patient with GCS 13-15 typically does not enlarge and may not need repeat head CT.
- [Both] Surgically relevant features include maximum hematoma thickness of 10 mm or greater, midline shift, and clinical deterioration.
- [Both] Mortality is high, and hematoma volume greater than 8-10% of intracranial volume is lethal. Emergency preoperative high-dose mannitol may improve outcome in selected surgical cases.
- [STATdx] STATdx supplemental captions emphasize that severe mass effect, edema, subfalcine/transtentorial herniation, obstructed foramen of Monro, and temporal horn displacement are poor-prognosis findings.
