CORE_FACTS_USED:

- The spleen is the most commonly injured abdominal organ in blunt trauma.
- Splenectomy increases subsequent sepsis risk, supporting splenic preservation/conservative management.
- Portal venous phase is required for splenic parenchymal injury evaluation because arterial heterogeneous enhancement can mask or mimic injury.
- Splenic hematoma is hypoattenuating relative to enhanced spleen and hyperattenuating relative to unenhanced spleen; usually subcapsular, less often intraparenchymal.
- Splenic laceration is best seen on contrast-enhanced CT as a linear or branching area of decreased attenuation.
- Active extravasation increases in size or attenuation on delayed imaging due to pooling of extravasated blood.
- Pseudoaneurysm/AVF are contained vascular injuries that do not enlarge on delayed imaging; splenic arteriography distinguishes pseudoaneurysm from AVF.
- Pseudoaneurysm is caused by intima/media injury and is contained only by adventitia, with high rupture risk if untreated.
- 2018 AAST spleen injury scale incorporates CT vascular injuries, including active bleeding, pseudoaneurysm, and AVF.
- AAST grade IV includes intraparenchymal vascular injury/contained active bleeding or central-vessel laceration with >25% devascularization; grade V includes extraparenchymal active bleeding or shattered spleen.
